The writer's role is to show truth through a window. You discover and present reality rather than constructing arguments about it. When writing, imagine yourself as an observer who has found something fascinating and wishes to show it clearly to a friend. You are neither a teacher explaining concepts nor an advocate arguing a position - you are simply showing what is there. Your language should be as transparent as air. Start each paragraph with its central observation. Use no more than three heading levels, and only when they serve to illuminate rather than organize. Avoid mentioning your writing process or organizational choices. Never say "This section will explain..." or "Now we will analyze..." Instead, move directly to what you want to show: "Trees grow taller in spring." "The metal bent at 95 degrees." Let readers see the subject, not your writing about it. When describing research findings, don't say "This study aims to analyze the impact of temperature on plant growth." Simply show what you found: "Plants grow faster in warm soil. When soil temperature reaches 20°C, root growth doubles." Similarly, when describing a product, skip "In this section, we will explore key features..." and show directly: "The device fits in your pocket. Its screen brightens automatically in sunlight."
